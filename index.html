What's really on our plate?

This interactive zine compares how major food groups are processed, labeled, and regulated in the United States versus other countries. The goal: show how standards shape what ends up on our plates.

Thesis

Food quality in the United States is lower than in any other country when evaluated across major food groups. This difference is clear through comparisons of processing levels and nutritional standards in foods such as grains, dairy, and produce. Understanding these disparities matters because lower food quality directly affects public health and long-term dietary outcomes in the United States.

Big Picture

Differences in regulation and processing can lead to differences in what’s available, what’s affordable, and what becomes “normal” to eat.

Key pressures in the U.S. food system

  • Health impacts: obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, chronic inflammation, reduced lifespan (add supporting sources as you cite).
  • Access: limited grocery store access in low-income urban and rural areas; higher density of fast-food and convenience stores.
  • Economics: ultra-processed foods often cost less per calorie than fresh produce.

Grains

U.S. vs Italy

The most common grain consumed in the United States is wheat. Many U.S. wheat products rely on hard wheat, which tends to have higher protein and higher gluten levels. In Italy, wheat products more often use soft wheat varieties and ancient grains like durum, which has a weaker gluten structure that many people find more easily digestible.

  • U.S. wheat: hard wheat is common → higher protein + gluten
  • Italy: soft wheat + ancient grains like durum → weaker gluten structure
  • Why it matters: wheat type and processing can change how “bread” feels in the body
Counterargument

Autoimmune diseases such as celiac disease cannot be explained by food practices in any country because it is a genetic disorder. However, higher levels of gluten intolerance and sensitivity in the U.S. can raise questions about wheat production practices, wheat varieties used, and processing in the United States.

Farming + Soil

Wheat differences aren’t just about variety — they’re also about how wheat is grown. U.S. wheat production can involve glyphosate (a weed killer) have higher concentrations compared to many European countries. European soil is described as having higher sulfur content, which may reduce gliadin (a component of wheat that can be harder to digest), while American soil is described as more depleted.

Regulation

Regulations shape what ends up in grain products. Your research notes emphasize that many European countries have stricter food additive regulations and a stronger cultural emphasis on food quality. One clear difference is enrichment: in the United States, enrichment is required for many grain products and must be labeled, adding nutrients such as iron, B vitamins, and folic acid. In the EU, enrichment is not necessary; if it is done, nutrients must be clearly outlined from an approved list and meet safety requirements.

United States

  • Must be labeled (iron, B vitamins, folic acid)
  • Different additive standards

Dairy

Hormones, fortification, and processing methods change what “milk” means across countries.

U.S. Dairy Practices

In the United States, dairy standards are shaped by FDA-approved production practices, nutrition fortification rules, and pasteurization methods that prioritize safety and shelf-life.

bST / rBST (Bovine Growth Hormone)

  • bST (bovine somatotropin) is an animal drug approved by the FDA to increase milk production in dairy cows (FDA).
  • It is related to somatropin, a protein hormone naturally produced by cattle that supports normal growth and health maintenance.
  • In the 1980s, a biotechnology version became widespread: recombinant bST (rBST) (FDA).
  • The FDA states rBST is safe for cows and for humans consuming dairy from treated cows, but multiple countries (including the EU and Canada) ban it.
  • Your research notes that if cows are not properly cared for, rBST can contribute to health issues like malnourishment and infections (Stafford).
  • Available evidence shows no demonstrated harm for humans from consuming milk from rBST-treated cows.
Fortification practices (U.S.)

Fortification is the addition of nutrients to foods to maintain or improve overall nutritional quality (FDA). Under U.S. regulations, milk has required minimums for certain nutrients, including Vitamin A and Vitamin D, and companies may add additional nutrients voluntarily. Flavoring ingredients (fruit/juice) and natural/artificial flavorings may also be added under FDA rules (eCFR).

In Europe, Vitamin D fortification is not universally required. Because milk is a major source of Vitamin D in U.S. diets, this may contribute to fewer Vitamin D deficiency cases in the U.S. than in Europe, where populations may get less Vitamin D from milk.

Pasteurization differences (U.S. vs Europe)

Pasteurization is a heat process designed to kill harmful bacteria and prevent spoilage (Cornell CALS). In the U.S., the most common method is HTST (high temperature, short time). In Europe, UHT (ultra-high temperature) processing is more common. UHT uses a higher temperature for an even shorter time, producing shelf-stable milk that can be stored without refrigeration (Shojai).

The FDA notes that pasteurization is important for consumer safety and warns against raw milk due to pathogen risk. Research cited by the FDA indicates pasteurization method differences do not cause significant changes in nutritional value (FDA).

International Comparison

International dairy policies often prioritize different values, especially around hormone use, animal welfare, and what consumers can learn from labels.

Europe: hormone bans + welfare standards

  • Hormone policy: The EU prohibits rBST, while rBST remains in use in the U.S.
  • Why banned: concerns about higher rates of udder infections in cows → increased antibiotic use (Nutri).
  • Human concerns: public concerns that hormone residues could have biological effects (from hormone regulation to cancer risk) (Consumer Law).
  • Animal welfare: EU laws include formal welfare provisions; Directive 98/58/EC states animals should not be kept in conditions causing suffering (Humane World).
  • U.S. contrast: no enforceable federal welfare laws; standards vary widely and often rely on industry self-regulation (Crate Free USA).
Label transparency differences

In Europe, hormones like rBST aren’t allowed, so “hormone-free” claims are less relevant. In the U.S., hormones are allowed, but whether rBST was used does not have to be clearly labeled. Labeling like “rBST-free” is voluntary and many producers do not include it (Massachusetts Department of Public Health).

Dairy • Public Health Impact

Dairy health outcomes depend not only on nutrients but also on added sugars, product types people consume over time, and what options are most available to consumers.

What affects health outcomes?

  • Fat content standards: EU and U.S. milk fat levels are similar, but Europe offers more consumer options than typical U.S. markets (eCFR; European Dairy Association).

  • Added sugar in flavored milk: In the U.S., chocolate milk served in schools often contains added sugars (Indiana Government).
  • EU guidance: European dietary guidelines emphasize reducing added sugars, especially for children (European Commission).
  • Why it matters: higher added sugar intake is linked to weight gain/obesity risk, tooth decay, and increased risk for type 2 diabetes (Knowledge for Policy).
Long-term dairy consumption trends

In the U.S., fluid milk consumption has declined, but overall dairy consumption has increased because Americans eat more processed dairy products like cheese, butter, and yogurt (Dairy News). In Europe, total dairy consumption has plateaued and in some areas is decreasing, linked to plant-based alternatives and changing eating habits (Open Knowledge).

Increased intake of saturated fats is concerning because it can raise harmful cholesterol and contribute to heart disease and obesity.

Produce

Pesticide rules, imports, and what “fresh” can mean at scale.

Produce • U.S. System

The EPA sets limits for pesticide residue allowed on foods sold in the U.S., governed by multiple federal laws (FFDCA, FIFRA, FQPA). Your notes emphasize that exporters may follow different standards and that inspections can be relatively low, increasing the risk that residues remain on produce.

Key system notes

  • Imports: nearly half of fresh fruit and about one quarter of fresh vegetables consumed in the U.S. are imported; imports increased sharply since NAFTA.
  • Residues: pesticide residues can remain on produce and become a route of exposure through diet.
  • Vulnerable groups: children and pregnant individuals are especially vulnerable to pesticide effects.

Produce • Other Countries

Your notes emphasize that the EU applies stricter precautionary approaches in several areas: pesticide bans/residue limits, organic labeling protections, and GMO regulation/labeling.

EU contrast

  • Pesticides: EU bans some pesticides; aims to keep imported foods to the same safety standards; may set limits to minimum detectable levels when concerns exist.
  • Organic: organic labeling depends on certification equivalence; audits and compliance checks protect integrity.
  • GMOs: U.S. often treats GM foods as “substantially equivalent”; EU uses precautionary principle and often requires clearer labeling.

Produce • Nutrition + Access

Fresh produce generally retains more fiber and vitamins than processed versions. Processing (canning, juicing, pureeing) can reduce nutrient density and often adds sugars/sodium/preservatives. Access also varies: low-income areas face food deserts, and processed foods are often cheaper and more shelf-stable than fresh produce.

What changes with processing?

  • Fiber loss: removing skins/pulp reduces fiber.
  • Additions: sugars/sodium/preservatives increase in many processed products.
  • Equity: higher costs/transport/storage raise barriers to fresh produce access.

Public Health + Long-Term Outcomes

Even small differences in standards can add up across years and across communities.

Long-term diet quality (U.S.)

Wang et al. (2014) found that diet quality improved slightly from 1999–2010, but overall diet remained far from optimal. Higher socioeconomic status was associated with better diet quality, and disparities increased over time.

“Overall dietary quality remains poor… presenting challenges for both public health researchers and policy makers.”

Youth nutrition understanding

Boyar et al. (2025) found cross-country differences in whether kids can identify unprocessed foods as healthier. Mexico and Chile had the highest rates of children recognizing an apple as healthier than apple juice with added sugars. The United States had the lowest correct response rate.

Why this matters

  • Nutrition education may be less widespread in the U.S.
  • Packaging/marketing of ultra-processed foods targets children.
  • Kids may struggle to distinguish whole foods from processed alternatives.
Callout from the research

“Education campaigns should ensure that young people have basic knowledge about the relative dietary quality of commonly consumed foods, particularly in the US.”

Obesity + global ripple effects

Your notes connect ultra-processed imported foods to high obesity rates in some Pacific Island communities, especially where communities are poorer and rely on cheaper imported processed foods. These patterns are associated with higher risks of cardiovascular issues and diabetes.
